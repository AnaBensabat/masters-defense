Cell Deformation

Cell deformation is essential to a variety of biological processes. Irregularities in cell deformation and shape can lead to illness and disease [1].

Cells deform in response to:

  • External and internal mechanical cues
  • External and internal biochemical cues

Cells deformation is essential for:

  • migration
  • division
  • differentiation
  • migration, invasion and metastasis of cancerous cells
[1] B. Alberts, et al. "Molecular Biology of the cell: seventh international student edition with registration card" WW Norton & Company, 2022. Chap. 10, 16

Cell Membrane and Cytoskeleton

Cell deformation is due to the action of both the cell membrane and the cell cytoskeleton [1].

The cell membrane is a complex and dynamic environment. It possesses proteins that:

  • serve as receptors
  • connect to the cytoskeleton
  • mediate interactions with the environment

Actin Filaments

  • motion, contractility and mechanical resistance
  • give cells strength and shape

Microtubules

  • provide strength and stability
  • essential in cell division
  • "cell railway": transport of organelles and other structures

Intermediate Filaments

  • provide structural integrity to the cell
  • key in the response to mechanical stress, maintain cell shape and anchor organelles

[1] B. Alberts, et al. "Molecular Biology of the cell: seventh international student edition with registration card" WW Norton & Company, 2022. Chap. 10, 16

Nuclear deformations on microgroove substrates

The nucleus is the hub of gene expression. How can deformations affect it?

Alterations in the nuclear mechanical properties have been linked with different pathologies and gene expression of stem cells, leading to alterations in development and cell differentiation.

  • Leclech et al. studied nuclear deformation of endothelial cells on microgroove substrates.
  • Deformations lead to partial and complete nuclear caging.
  • Caging is driven by adhesion without cytoskeleton-associated forces.

Goal: Development of a multi-phase-field model that highlights adhesion forces and relative nuclear stiffness to support experimental findings and gain insight on nuclear deformation

[3] C. Leclech et al. “Micro-Scale Topography Triggers Dynamic 3D Nuclear Deformations”. en. In: Advanced Science (Jan. 2025)

Nuclear deformations on microgroove substrates

This system was simulated via a multi-phase-field model where the substrate, the nucleus and the cell are each described by a different field.

The free energy now also features terms for the nucleus:
\[ \begin{align*} \quad F[\phi,\nu,\psi] &= \int \kappa_\text{C}\left(\frac{\varepsilon}{2}|\nabla \phi|^2 + \frac{1}{4}\phi^2(1-\phi)^2\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \kappa_\text{N}\left(\frac{\varepsilon}{2}|\nabla \nu|^2 + \frac{1}{4}\nu^2(1-\nu)^2\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \eta\left( \nabla h(\phi)\cdot\nabla h(\psi) \right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \gamma\left( h(\phi)\cdot h(\psi) + h(1-\phi)\cdot h(\nu)\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r} \\ &+ \alpha_V (V_\text{target,C}-V[\phi])^2 + \alpha_V (V_\text{target,N}-V[\nu])^2\\ \end{align*} \]
The equation of motion features a novel term that maintains cell integrity:

\[ \begin{align*} g[\phi]=-\chi\phi(1-\phi)\int_\mathcal{S} \Theta(-\nabla \phi(\mathbf{r})\cdot\nabla\phi(\mathbf{r}')) (\nabla \phi(\mathbf{r})\cdot \nabla \phi(\mathbf{r}'))e^{-|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|/\delta}\text{d}\mathbf{r}' \end{align*} \]

Modelling the malaria parasite pitting

During a key stage of its life cycle, the malaria parasite develops inside human red blood cells (RBC). The human spleen is capable of mechanically extracting the parasites in a process generally called pitting [4]].

To study the pitting in vitro A. Elizalde-Torrent et al. developed microfluidic devices that mimick splenic filtration and are able to remove Plasmodium parasite.

The parasite can be removed solely by mechanical stress on the RBC, without affecting the cell integrity [5].

Goal: Development of a multi-phase-field model replicates and expands upon experimental conditions to find the optimal conditions for pitting.

[4] R. E. Mebius and G. Kraal. “Structure and function of the spleen”. In: Nature reviews immunology 5.8 (2005) [5] A. Elizalde-Torrent et al. “Pitting of malaria parasites in microfluidic devices mimicking spleen interendothelial slits”. In: Scientific reports 11.1 (2021)

Modelling the malaria parasite pitting

This system was simulated via a multi-phase-field model where the channel, the parasite and the RBC are each described by a different field.

The free energy is analogous to that of the previous system: \[ \begin{align*} \quad F[\phi,\nu,\psi] &= \int \kappa_\text{RBC} \left(\frac{\varepsilon}{2}|\nabla \phi|^2 + \frac{1}{4}\phi^2(1-\phi)^2\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \kappa_\text{P} \left(\frac{\varepsilon}{2}|\nabla \nu|^2 + \frac{1}{4}\nu^2(1-\nu)^2\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \eta\left( \nabla h(\phi)\cdot\nabla h(\psi) \right)\text{d}\mathbf{r}\\ &+ \int \gamma\left( h(\phi)\cdot h(\psi) + h(1-\phi)\cdot h(\nu)\right)\text{d}\mathbf{r} \\ &+ \alpha_V (V_\text{target,RBC}-V[\phi])^2 + \alpha_V (V_\text{target,P}-V[\nu])^2\\ \end{align*} \] The equation of the evolution now features an advection velocity term: \[ \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} + \mathbf{v}\cdot \nabla \phi = -M\frac{\delta F[\phi]}{\delta \phi} \end{align*} \]

CONCLUSIONS

  • There is an optimal channel geometry for pitting
  • Critical velocity for pitting onset
  • RBC-channel adhesion inhibits pitting
  • Pitting is independent of parasite stiffness

Multiscale model for the cytoskeleton

In the final project of this system, we studied a system of active Brownian particles (ABPs) coupled with a phase-field vesicle.

The free energy is: \[ F[\phi] = \int_V \left(\frac{\varepsilon}{2} (\nabla \phi)^2 + \frac{1}{4}\phi^2(1-\phi)^2\right) \text{d}\mathbf{r} + \alpha_V(V-V_\text{target})^2 \] and the equation of motion is: \[ \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} + \alpha_CC|\nabla\phi| + \phi(1-\phi)(\mathbf{u}\cdot \nabla \phi) = -M\frac{\delta F[\phi]}{\delta \phi}\] where [6]: \[C=-\nabla\cdot\frac{\nabla \phi}{|\nabla\phi|}.\] and \[ \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{r}_i) = \sum_i^N A \mathbf{v}_i\int_C e^{-(\mathbf{r}'-\mathbf{r}_i)^2/\delta^2} \text{d}\mathbf{r}' \]

The particles are modeled by a Langevin equation: \[ m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_i = -\alpha \mathbf{\dot{r}}_i - \sum_{i\neq j} \nabla U(|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j|) + \mathbf{F}_{a,i} + \mathbf{F}_{m,i} + \xi_i \] where the repulsion at the membrane is given by: \[ F_\text{m,i}=\frac{F}{h(\phi)}\nabla\phi(\mathbf{r}_i) \] The active force is modeled as: \[ \mathbf{F}_\text{a,i} = f_p \mathbf{e}_i \] where [7]: \[ \begin{gather} \dot{\theta_i} = D_r\cot\theta_i + \eta_{\theta_i} \\ \dot{\varphi_i} = \frac{1}{\sin\theta_i}\eta_{\varphi_i} \end{gather} \] and \[ \begin{gather} \left<\eta_\xi(t)\right> = 0 \\ \left<\eta_\xi(t)\eta_{\xi\prime}(t\prime)\right> = 2D_r\delta_{\xi\xi\prime}\delta(t-t\prime) \end{gather} \]

[6] T. Biben, K. Kassner, and C. Misbah. “Phase-field approach to three-dimensional vesicle dynamics”. In: Physical Review E 72.4 (2005) [7] M. Raible and A. Engel. “Langevin equation for the rotation of a magnetic particle”. In: Applied organometallic chemistry 18.10 (2004)

Multiscale model for the cytoskeleton

A study of the dynamics of 3D ABPs in bulk.

The mean squared displacement (MSD) matches the theoretical predictions [8]. In the dilute regime $(\phi_P = 0.004)$:

\[D^\text{eff}_t=D^0_t + \frac{f_p^2}{d(d-1)\alpha_t^2D_r}\]

Higher density regimes were explored for different levels of activity.
  • A single peak matching the global density for low $ \phi_P $ - dilute phase
  • Transition from a single peak to bimodal PDF as activity increases - phase coexistence (MIPS)

\[ \begin{align*} \phi_P &\text{ - Packing fraction} \\ &\text{(density)} \end{align*} \] \[ \begin{align*} \text{Pe} &\text{ - Péclet number} \\ &\text{(activity)} \end{align*} \]

[8] J. Elgeti, R. G. Winkler, and G. Gompper. “Physics of microswimmers—single particle motion and collective behavior: a review”. In: Reports on progress in physics 78.5 (2015)

Multiscale model for the cytoskeleton

Study of the ABPs dynamics under rigid and spherical confinement.

The MSD shows an initial ballistic regime and plateaus as the particles reach the potential wall.

Higher density regimes were explored for different levels of activity.
  • A single peak matching the global density for low $ \phi_P $ - gas phase
  • Peak broadening and shift with increasing Pe - accumulation at the boundary and layer formation
  • Hexatic ordering at the boundary at high $\phi_P$

Multiscale model for the cytoskeleton

Study of the ABPs dynamics under rigid and spherical confinement.

Membrane deformations are quantified via the roughness:

\[ \mathcal{R} = \frac{\sqrt{\text{Var(r(t))}}}{\left< r(t) \right> } \]

A larger repulsion force coefficient results in larger deformations for high Pe.

The local density PDF was studied for the high deformation regime:

  • A sharp peak for low Pe - nearly uniform accumulation at the boundary
  • Broad peak for high Pe - coexistence of regions with different densities
